Canada MSB Compliance Program 2026: FINTRAC 5-Pillar Framework

FINTRAC's MSB compliance framework is built on five mandatory pillars. Skip any one and your registration fails or your examination produces findings. This is the practical guide for building a compliance program that survives FINTRAC examinations and satisfies banking partners — without overspending on consultant boilerplate.

The 5 pillars in detail

Pillar 1: Policies and procedures

Written, kept up to date, addressing every MSB activity you conduct. For crypto MSBs (DCS), this typically runs 40–80 pages covering:

  • Customer onboarding (KYC / ID verification / proof of address)
  • Customer Due Diligence (CDD) and Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)
  • Source-of-funds verification (especially for crypto deposits — blockchain analytics)
  • Transaction monitoring rules and escalation procedures
  • Suspicious activity identification and SAR escalation
  • Sanctions screening (SEMA, UN, OFAC at minimum)
  • Record-keeping (5-year minimum, transaction history searchable)
  • Reporting workflows (LCTR, LVCTR, EFTR, STR, TPR)
  • Travel Rule procedures (crypto transfers ≥$1,000 CAD)
  • Customer offboarding and account closure procedures

Critical: Generic templates fail FINTRAC examinations. Each policy must reference your specific business — your customer types, geographies, products, transaction volumes. Reviewers spot template language immediately.

Pillar 2: Risk assessment

Documented analysis of money-laundering and terrorist-financing risks specific to YOUR business. This is the most-failed pillar — generic risk assessments are the #1 cause of FINTRAC examination findings.

A FINTRAC-acceptable risk assessment covers four risk dimensions:

  1. Customer risk: What types of customers do you serve? PEPs? High-net-worth? Retail? Customer geography? Each customer segment gets a risk rating.
  2. Geographic risk: What countries do customers and counterparties operate in? FATF grey/black-listed countries (currently Iran, North Korea, etc.) require enhanced due diligence.
  3. Product/service risk: What MSB activities do you conduct? Crypto exchange (high risk)? FX (medium)? Money transfer (medium-high)? Privacy coins (very high)?
  4. Channel risk: How do customers interact? In-person (lower risk) vs online-only (higher risk)? Anonymous wallet deposits (highest risk)?

Each dimension produces a score; combined risk score determines monitoring intensity, KYC depth, and reporting frequency.

Pillar 3: Compliance officer

FINTRAC requires a named individual at senior level with documented authority to enforce the AML program. Junior staff fail this test.

Compliance officer requirements:

  • Senior position — director, CCO, or general manager (not junior staff)
  • Knowledge of PCMLTFA, FINTRAC guidance, and the MSB's specific risks
  • Authority to stop transactions, escalate to board, file SARs without management override
  • Available for FINTRAC examinations and bank queries
  • Recognized AML training within 6 months: CAMS (ACAMS, ~$1,500, 8-12 weeks) or ICA Diploma in AML (~$2,500, 6 months) most common

Three operational models:

  1. Founder/director as compliance officer. Cheapest. Works for early-stage MSBs. Founder takes CAMS training.
  2. Outsourced fractional compliance officer. $2,000–$8,000/year. Provider firms specialize in MSB compliance. Useful for growing operators not yet ready for full-time hire.
  3. Full-time dedicated compliance officer. $30,000–$80,000/year. Required for institutional-scale MSBs handling $10M+ annual transactions.

Pillar 4: Training program

Documented training for ALL employees and agents on AML obligations and the company's specific procedures. Annual minimum; new-hire training mandatory.

Training program must cover:

  • PCMLTFA + FINTRAC obligations overview
  • Company-specific policies and procedures
  • Customer due diligence process (KYC, EDD)
  • How to identify suspicious activity
  • Internal escalation procedures (when and how to flag)
  • Record-keeping and confidentiality requirements
  • Sanctions screening obligations

Training must be documented (attendance records, training material versions, completion dates). FINTRAC examiners review training records during examinations.

Pillar 5: Effectiveness review

Independent review of the AML program every 2 years, in writing. The most-skipped pillar — many MSBs forget until FINTRAC examination triggers it.

Effectiveness review specifics:

  • Independent — cannot be performed by the compliance officer themselves. Outsourced to an external firm or internal audit team unrelated to AML.
  • Comprehensive scope — reviews policies, risk assessment, training, transaction monitoring effectiveness, reporting timeliness
  • Written report — formal document with findings, recommendations, remediation timeline
  • Frequency: at minimum every 2 years; many MSBs do annual
  • Cost: $3,000–$10,000 typically for outsourced review of a small-mid MSB

Every MSB submits five report types as triggered. For crypto MSBs (DCS), LVCTR is the most frequent.

Travel Rule for crypto MSBs (DCS specific)

Since 2021, Canadian crypto MSBs must comply with the Travel Rule for crypto transfers ≥$1,000 CAD. Originator and beneficiary information must be transmitted to the receiving VASP/MSB.

Required information:

  • Originator name, address, account number (or wallet address)
  • Beneficiary name, account number (or wallet address)
  • Originator's identity verification status

Common Travel Rule tooling: Notabene, Sumsub Travel Rule, VerifyVASP, TRP Labs ($1,000–$10,000/year depending on volume). Failure to comply triggers FINTRAC enforcement.

FAQ — Canada MSB compliance program

What are the 5 pillars of a FINTRAC MSB compliance program?

FINTRAC requires every MSB compliance program to have: (1) Policies and procedures — written, kept up to date, addressing all MSB activities; (2) Risk assessment — documented analysis of money laundering and terrorist financing risks specific to the business; (3) Compliance officer — named senior individual with authority to enforce the program; (4) Training program — documented training for all employees and agents; (5) Effectiveness review — independent review of the AML program every 2 years, in writing.

How much does a Canada MSB compliance program cost?

$5,000–$15,000 to build the initial documented program. Annual maintenance $3,000–$8,000 plus compliance officer fees ($2,000–$8,000/year for outsourced fractional or $30K–$80K full-time hire). Crypto MSBs (DCS) need additional transaction monitoring software ($5K+/year) and Travel Rule tooling ($1K–$10K/year).

What reports does a Canadian MSB submit to FINTRAC?

Five report types: LCTR (cash ≥$10K CAD, 15 days), LVCTR (crypto ≥$10K CAD, 5 business days), EFTR (international EFT ≥$10K CAD, 5 working days), STR (suspicious, 30 days from identification), TPR (terrorist property, immediately).

Who can serve as MSB compliance officer?

Must be at senior level (director, CCO, GM — not junior staff), with knowledge of PCMLTFA + FINTRAC guidance, documented authority to enforce the program, and recognized AML training within 6 months (CAMS or ICA Diploma standard). Founder/director, employee, or outsourced fractional compliance officer all acceptable.

How often must the effectiveness review happen?

Every 2 years minimum. Many MSBs do annual. Must be performed by someone independent of the compliance officer (external firm or internal audit unrelated to AML). Written report with findings and recommendations. Cost: $3,000–$10,000 for a small-mid MSB outsourced review.
