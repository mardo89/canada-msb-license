Why US money transmitter licensing is so expensive

The US has no single federal money-transmitter framework. Instead, every operator serving US customers must navigate FinCEN MSB registration (federal AML) plus state-by-state money transmitter licensing (state consumer protection). The state component is the cost driver:

  • 49 separate state licenses — Montana is the only state without an MTL requirement.
  • Surety bonds vary $10,000–$7,000,000 per state — California requires up to $2M; Washington requires $10K–$550K; New York is heaviest.
  • Application fees $1,500–$25,000+ per state — plus annual renewal fees.
  • State-specific capital requirements — many states require $100K–$500K minimum tangible net worth.
  • NY BitLicense — separate framework for crypto businesses serving NY customers; $5,000 application but 18+ months review and ~$50K–$200K compliance overhead.
  • Each state license is annual — ongoing fees of $1,000–$10,000+ per state per year.

For a US operator licensed in 30 states (covering ~85% of US population), realistic year-1 cost is $400K–$800K. Full 49-state coverage runs $700K–$1.5M+.

Why Canada MSB is so cheap

Canada is the opposite: one federal MSB registration with FINTRAC covers the entire country. No state-by-state. No surety bonds. No minimum capital. The only costs are compliance work (AML program, compliance officer, internal procedures) and optional Canadian incorporation.

  • $0 FINTRAC government fee
  • $0 minimum capital
  • $0 surety bond
  • 2-year renewal cycle (free)
  • One license, one regulator, one country

When Canada MSB wins

  1. Crypto exchanges with global customer base. Canada MSB lets you serve Canadian and most international customers (subject to home-country rules) at a fraction of US cost. Most international crypto exchanges target Canada MSB and avoid US licensing entirely.
  2. Fast launch matters. 3–6 weeks vs 12–24 months. If you need to be operational this quarter, Canada is the only realistic regulated North American option.
  3. Capital-efficient operators. $8K–$25K vs $250K–$1M+ frees up $200K–$975K for actual business operations.
  4. FX dealers and money transmitters with sub-$10M annual volume. US licensing fixed costs make sub-scale US operators uneconomic.
  5. Crypto-to-fiat operations needing CAD/USD rails. Canada banking is mature for MSB-licensed operators and supports both currencies.

When US licensing wins (despite the cost)

  1. Material US retail revenue. If 30%+ of revenue is from US customers, US licensing is non-negotiable. Operating without it is illegal and carries SEC, CFTC, and state-AG enforcement risk.
  2. Brand-driven public exchanges. Coinbase, Kraken, Gemini, Crypto.com all hold US state MTLs. The badge matters for institutional onboarding and IPO/M&A optionality.
  3. NY-specific operations. NY BitLicense is the gold standard for crypto credibility. Worth pursuing for operators specifically targeting NY institutional clients.
  4. Fiat payment processing at scale. US payment-processor partnerships (Stripe, Plaid, etc.) often require US-licensed MSB status.

Realistic strategy: Most international crypto exchanges launch with Canada MSB to capture North American volume cost-effectively, then add US licensing in 3–5 highest-value states (CA, NY via BitLicense, TX, FL, IL) once US revenue justifies the spend. Full 49-state coverage is a $1M+ commitment — only worth it for established operators with $10M+ US revenue.

FAQ — Canada MSB vs USA

Is Canada MSB cheaper than US money transmitter license?

Yes — dramatically. Canada MSB costs $8,000–$25,000 in year one with $0 government fee and $0 minimum capital. US money transmitter licensing requires FinCEN MSB registration ($0) plus state-by-state MTLs across 49 states (excluding Montana), with surety bonds of $10,000–$7,000,000 per state and individual state fees of $1,500–$25,000+ per state. Total US setup typically runs $250,000–$1,000,000+ over 12–24 months.

Can a Canada MSB serve US customers?

Generally no. US customers trigger FinCEN MSB registration plus state money transmitter licensing in any state where customers reside. A Canada MSB cannot legally onboard US retail customers without separate US authorizations. Some Canada MSBs serve US institutional or high-net-worth clients via specific exemptions, but US retail requires US licensing.

How long does US money transmitter licensing take vs Canada MSB?

Canada MSB: 3–6 weeks (FINTRAC pre-registration). US: 12–24 months for full multi-state coverage (FinCEN MSB ~30 days; state MTLs 6–18 months each, often filed in parallel). Many crypto operators start with Canada MSB for North American operations, then layer US licensing only for the highest-value US states (NY BitLicense, CA, TX) — not all 49.

What's the deal with NY BitLicense?

The NY BitLicense (NYDFS Virtual Currency License) is a crypto-specific framework separate from standard US MTL. $5,000 application fee, 18+ month review, $100K+ initial capital, ~$50K–$200K compliance overhead. As of 2026, ~30 firms hold BitLicense. It's the most respected US state crypto credential — worth pursuing for operators specifically targeting NY institutional clients, but not for general US market access.

Can I avoid US licensing entirely as a crypto exchange?

Yes — by not serving US persons. Many international crypto exchanges (e.g., Bybit, KuCoin historically) blocked US IP addresses and required attestations to avoid US licensing. This is operationally workable but reduces TAM by ~25–30%. Geographic blocking + KYC US-resident screening is the standard approach.

What about FinCEN MSB without state MTLs?

FinCEN MSB registration alone covers federal AML obligations but does NOT authorize you to provide money-transmission services to US customers. State MTLs are separate and required for actual customer-facing operations. Some operators register FinCEN MSB for AML-compliance signaling without state MTLs — but they still cannot legally serve US retail.
